# Alternative names for IEF

Date: 2026-03-15

## Goal

Suggest alternative names for IEF in two styles:

1. names that explain the system directly
2. names that feel more like short product/tool names with a bit more character

The second group is intentionally closer to the naming style used in Peter Steinberger projects: short, memorable, tool-like, slightly metaphorical.

## What the current name does well

`Idea Execution Framework` is:

- clear
- descriptive
- easy to explain
- good as an internal working title

Its weakness is mostly branding:

- it sounds like a methodology document more than a product
- `framework` makes it feel abstract and a bit heavy
- `idea execution` is accurate but not especially memorable

## Explicit names

These stay close to what the project actually is.

### 1. Idea Loop

Why it works:
- simple and short
- captures the recurring human-agent cycle
- easier to say than `Idea Execution Framework`

Trade-off:
- less explicit about repo memory and structure

### 2. Repo Loop

Why it works:
- emphasizes the repo as the operating surface
- short and practical

Trade-off:
- slightly too generic on its own

### 3. Execution Loop

Why it works:
- reflects the iterative task rhythm well
- still clear and technical

Trade-off:
- broad enough that it may collide conceptually with other systems

### 4. Repo Memory Loop

Why it works:
- captures the two most distinctive IEF ideas: repo memory and iteration

Trade-off:
- more explanatory than elegant

### 5. Project Memory Loop

Why it works:
- slightly broader and friendlier than `Repo Memory Loop`

Trade-off:
- less specific about Git as the actual surface

### 6. Commit Loop

Why it works:
- ties directly to the Git-backed work rhythm
- compact and memorable

Trade-off:
- over-emphasizes commits relative to planning and memory

### 7. Worktree Loop

Why it works:
- technically precise for Git-oriented users
- more distinctive than `Repo Loop`

Trade-off:
- a bit too Git-insider for broader audiences

### 8. Execution Ledger

Why it works:
- emphasizes durable tracked history in the repo

Trade-off:
- sounds slightly formal or accounting-like

### 9. Project Workbench

Why it works:
- suggests a place where human and agent work together
- practical and approachable

Trade-off:
- less emphasis on loop/process

### 10. Repo Workbench

Why it works:
- combines concreteness with a more product-like feel

Trade-off:
- still somewhat functional rather than distinctive

## More product-style names

These are less explicit but more name-like.

### 1. Foundry

Why it works:
- strong metaphor for turning rough ideas into concrete artifacts
- short and memorable

Trade-off:
- common product name, so differentiation could be hard

### 2. Relay

Why it works:
- suggests handoff between human and agent
- short, clean, modern

Trade-off:
- less obvious that the repo is the memory surface

### 3. Wayfinder

Why it works:
- implies navigation, direction, and guided execution
- feels like a real product name

Trade-off:
- slightly more exploratory than execution-oriented

### 4. Dockyard

Why it works:
- strong metaphor for building, refitting, and maintaining projects
- has some of the same concrete/tool vibe as names like `OpenClaw`

Trade-off:
- a bit heavier and more industrial

### 5. Harbor

Why it works:
- suggests a stable home for projects, memory, and agents
- simple and calm

Trade-off:
- more about storage and coordination than execution

### 6. Bridge

Why it works:
- suggests command, coordination, and visibility
- good fit if the system is seen as the place where human and agent meet

Trade-off:
- common name and highly overloaded

### 7. Flight Deck

Why it works:
- strong control-plane metaphor
- fits the idea of dispatching work across projects and agents

Trade-off:
- a bit more dramatic than the current project tone

### 8. Switchboard

Why it works:
- good for a system that routes work, memory, and decisions
- memorable and a bit unusual

Trade-off:
- emphasizes coordination more than repo memory

### 9. Workshop

Why it works:
- approachable, practical, and maker-oriented
- good match for iterative human-agent work

Trade-off:
- less distinctive than stronger metaphor names

### 10. Forge

Why it works:
- compact, active, and execution-oriented
- strong “turn plans into artifacts” feel

Trade-off:
- already used heavily in the wider developer ecosystem

### 11. Beacon

Why it works:
- suggests orientation, visibility, and steady guidance
- lighter and cleaner than some of the industrial names

Trade-off:
- less directly tied to execution work

### 12. Pilot House

Why it works:
- has the command-and-navigation flavor without sounding too abstract
- fits the human-agent steering model well

Trade-off:
- slightly longer and less crisp than one-word names

## Best shortlist

If the goal is to stay fairly explicit:

- `Idea Loop`
- `Repo Workbench`
- `Execution Ledger`
- `Repo Memory Loop`

If the goal is to move toward a more name-like product identity:

- `Foundry`
- `Relay`
- `Wayfinder`
- `Dockyard`
- `Harbor`

## My recommendation

If you want the safest evolution from `IEF`, I would shortlist:

1. `Idea Loop`
2. `Repo Workbench`
3. `Relay`
4. `Wayfinder`

If you want something closest to the naming energy of Peter Steinberger projects, I would shortlist:

1. `Relay`
2. `Foundry`
3. `Dockyard`
4. `Wayfinder`

## Naming direction suggestion

There are really two possible branding directions:

### Direction A — clear and method-like

Examples:
- `Idea Loop`
- `Repo Memory Loop`
- `Repo Workbench`

Best when:
- the project is mainly a methodology/framework
- clarity matters more than personality

### Direction B — tool-like and memorable

Examples:
- `Relay`
- `Foundry`
- `Wayfinder`
- `Dockyard`

Best when:
- you want the project to feel more like a named product
- you expect a family of related tools or conventions later

## Final note

Before choosing a final name, it would be worth checking:

- GitHub/repo naming collisions
- domain availability if that matters later
- trademark conflicts for the stronger one-word candidates
